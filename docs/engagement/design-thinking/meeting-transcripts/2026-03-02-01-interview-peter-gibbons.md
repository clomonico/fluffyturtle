## Peter Gibbons — Senior Process Analyst

**Date:** 2026-03-02
**Interviewer:** Discovery Team
**Stakeholder type:** Primary (daily user, high interest, lower power)

---

**Q: Thanks for sitting down with us, Peter. Can you tell me the story of the last approval that went sideways?**

**Peter:** Yeah. Last Tuesday. Marketing needed a $12K vendor payment expedited because the event was in five days. It sat in Samir's queue for two days because he was out and nobody else could approve finance items in that range. So I just... forwarded it to his backup with a note that said "Samir reviewed, please countersign." He hadn't reviewed it. But the event happened, so I guess it worked out.

---

**Q: That's interesting. So you created a workaround to keep things moving. How often does that kind of thing happen?**

**Peter:** Define "that kind of thing." If you mean me finding a way around the process so work doesn't stop... I don't know, a few times a week? It's not like I'm breaking rules. The rules just don't account for the fact that people go on PTO, or have 40 other things in their queue. Somebody has to keep the lights on.

---

**Q: When you say the process doesn't work, where does it actually stall?**

**Peter:** **"Honestly? The process works fine on paper. The problem is that nobody follows it, including me, and the only reason anything gets approved at all is because three of us have figured out how to route around the bottlenecks."** That's the thing nobody wants to say out loud. Lumbergh thinks we need a new system. We don't need a new system. We need fewer people in the chain. Half the approvers don't even read what they're signing. They just click approve because someone before them already did.

---

**Q: Bill mentioned that visibility is a big issue — that leadership can't see where things stand. Is that your experience too?**

**Peter:** Bill can't see where things stand because the thing he's looking at isn't what's actually happening. The SharePoint tracker says an approval is "in review with finance." What that actually means is I emailed Samir directly, he texted me back "looks fine," and I moved it to the next stage myself. So yeah, the tracker is up to date. It's just fiction. Bill's looking at a dashboard that shows a process that doesn't exist.

---

**Q: Tom Smykowski told us the approval workflow has important safeguards built in and that simplifying it would introduce risk. What do you think about that?**

**Peter:** Tom wrote that process in like 2019. He'll tell you every step matters. Here's what actually matters: legal review on anything over $50K, finance sign-off on anything hitting a new budget line, and department head approval. That's it. The other six steps are Tom making sure he has a reason to be in meetings. I'm not trying to be mean. That's just what it is. There's a step in there — step four — where the "process owner" reviews for "routing accuracy." That's Tom checking that I filled out the form correctly. I fill out the form. I am the routing. He's reviewing my work to make sure I gave it to him correctly. You see the problem.

---

**Q: Samir said he adds extra review cycles because he's worried about audit exposure. Does that match what you see?**

**Peter:** Samir's a good guy. He's also terrified. He adds a second review on anything over $10K even though policy only requires it over $50K. Nobody told him to do that. He just does it because one time in 2022 an AP clerk miscoded an expense and internal audit flagged it in Samir's queue. So now everything gets double-checked. That one incident created a permanent extra step for every request that touches finance. And nobody's ever going to tell him to stop because nobody wants to be the person who said "skip the review" right before something blows up.

---

**Q: If you could change one thing about the process tomorrow, what would it be?**

**Peter:** Cut the chain from nine approvers to three. That's it. Legal, finance, department head. Everyone else is either rubber-stamping or adding a step to justify their calendar. But that won't happen because Lumbergh wants a tool, not a conversation about why his process has nine steps. It's a lot easier to buy software than to tell Tom his job doesn't add value.

---

**Q: You mentioned you route around bottlenecks a few times a week. Do you track those workarounds anywhere?**

**Peter:** No. Why would I? If I documented every time I skipped a step or forged a soft approval to keep things moving, I'd basically be writing a confession. The whole point is that it's invisible. That's what makes it work. Look, I know that's not great from an audit perspective. But if I followed the actual process as written, turnaround would double overnight and Joanna's team would be dead in the water. Somebody would notice then. They'd just blame the new system instead of the old process.

---

*Post-interview note: Peter's undocumented workarounds represent a significant finding. The current process metrics (cycle time, completion rate) reflect his interventions, not the process design. Any new system built on current performance baselines will be benchmarking against a fiction. Additionally, his casual mention of forwarding approvals with implied sign-off ("Samir reviewed, please countersign") is an untracked control gap that audit would flag as a segregation-of-duties issue.*
