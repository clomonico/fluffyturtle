## Milton Waddams — Long-tenured Specialist

**Date:** 2026-03-02
**Interviewer:** Design Research Lead
**Context:** Simulated stakeholder interview for Approval Process Overhaul
**Persona profile:** Has been in the org forever. Knows the history of every approval exception and edge case. Wants to be heard and acknowledged. Holds institutional memory no one asks for.

---

**Q1: Milton, thanks for sitting down with me. Can you tell me a bit about your role in the approval process?**

Well, I... I've been here since 1996. Thirty years. I was here when we were still doing approvals on triplicate carbon forms, and I... I process the exception requests. The ones that don't fit the normal routing. I also... they moved my desk again, by the way. I used to be by the window on the fourth floor and now I'm in the basement next to the boiler and I... I was told I could listen to the radio at a reasonable volume but then they... anyway. Yes. Exceptions. I handle the exceptions. And the archive reconciliation. Nobody else knows how to do the archive reconciliation.

---

**Q2: That's a long time. When you say "exception requests," what kinds of things are we talking about?**

Oh, there are... there are quite a few. Procurement exceptions over $50,000 — those have a different routing since 2019. And capital expenditure approvals that cross fiscal year boundaries, those need dual sign-off because of... well, because of what happened. And there are the vendor exceptions for sole-source contracts, and the ones where a department head is also the budget holder, which creates a conflict-of-interest loop, and... I documented all of this. I have a binder. Well, I had a binder. Someone moved it when they moved my desk. But I also have it in a shared drive folder that... I don't think anyone else has access to. I requested access for the team in 2021 but nobody responded to the ticket.

---

**Q3: You mentioned something happened in 2019 that changed the procurement routing. Can you tell me about that?**

Oh. Yes. That was... we had a purchase order for $120,000 in networking equipment that got approved through the standard two-level flow. But the vendor had changed their payment terms and nobody caught it because the old approval template didn't have a field for payment terms review. So it got approved, the PO went out, the vendor shipped the equipment, and then accounts payable couldn't process the invoice because the payment terms didn't match our standard net-60. We ended up paying a $14,000 late fee and nearly lost the vendor relationship. That's why step 7 exists. I added step 7. Well, I recommended it. Tom said it was his idea in the write-up but... I was the one who found the problem. I always find the problems. Nobody ever... *[trails off, adjusts glasses]*

I was told there would be cake at that project retrospective, too, and there wasn't.

---

**Q4: That's really helpful context. So step 7 is specifically a payment terms validation?**

It's a payment terms and contract deviation check. It only triggers on purchase orders above $50,000 or when the vendor is flagged as "non-standard terms" in the vendor master. It takes about ten minutes if you know what you're looking for. I... I process about forty of those a month. Michael keeps saying that step is "redundant overhead" and he wants to eliminate it in his new system, but he... **he wasn't here when we ate that $14,000 penalty**. He's been here three years. He thinks everything before he arrived was just people being inefficient. But some of those steps are there because someone bled for them. I bled for that one. *[quietly]* Nobody remembers.

---

**Q5: It sounds like you feel your input hasn't been sought out in recent discussions about changing the process. Is that fair?**

*[long pause]*

**They didn't even invite me to the kickoff meeting.** I found out about it from the calendar invite that Joanna forwarded to me afterward. She felt bad. She's nice. But Bill — Lumbergh — he put together the invite list and I wasn't on it. Thirty years. I've processed more exceptions than anyone in this building. I know every edge case, every workaround, every reason why a step was added. And they didn't think to include me.

You know, Tom says he "owns" the approval workflow. He wrote the documentation, sure. But half of what's in that documentation is wrong. The flowchart on page 3 still shows the old three-level routing for capital expenditures, and that hasn't been accurate since we added the dual sign-off in 2019. I told him. He said he'd update it. That was four years ago. *[mutters]* He also took my stapler. The red Swingline. Said he needed it for a "process documentation session." That was in February. It's March.

---

**Q6: If you could change one thing about how the approval process works today, what would it be?**

I would... I would make the exception routing visible. Right now, when an approval hits an exception condition, it comes to me through email. Just email. There's no ticket, no tracking, no SLA. I process it and email back. If I'm out sick, nobody knows the exception is sitting there. Last August I had the flu for a week and seventeen exception requests just... sat. Nobody even noticed until a department head called Samir screaming about a stalled PO and Samir didn't know I was the one who was supposed to clear it because... **nobody documents that I'm in the process**. I'm not in the flowchart. I'm not in the RACI. I'm not in the system. I just... exist. In the basement. Processing things.

If they automate the process without accounting for exception routing, those seventeen requests won't just sit for a week. They'll disappear entirely. And nobody will know until the late fees start showing up.

---

**Q7: You mentioned Peter Gibbons earlier, and his workarounds. Do you see those in the exception flow?**

Oh, Peter. Peter is... Peter is very clever. I'll give him that. He figured out that if you enter a PO as two separate requests under $50,000 instead of one over $50,000, it bypasses the exception routing entirely. He's been doing that for at least a year. I don't think he realizes that also bypasses the conflict-of-interest check. So if a department head is splitting a purchase to avoid the threshold... there's no second set of eyes on it. I mentioned it to Tom once. Tom said it "wasn't a material risk." Tom doesn't know what he doesn't know.

I'm not saying Peter is doing anything wrong on purpose. He's just trying to get things done. But the system has a hole in it and I'm the only one who sees it because I'm the one who sees all the exceptions... and all the things that should be exceptions but aren't.

---

**Q8: Milton, this has been incredibly valuable. Is there anything else you want to make sure we capture before we wrap up?**

Just... just that I have a lot of documentation. The binder — well, the digital version of the binder. It has every exception case going back to 2014. Every edge case, every resolution, every time we added a step and why. If someone is going to redesign this process, they should read it. All of it. It's 340 pages but it's... it's organized. I organized it. Section tabs and everything.

And if you could... if you could mention to someone that I'd like my stapler back, I would appreciate that. It's the red one. It was mine from home. I bought it with my own money.

*[stands up, pauses at the door]*

Also — and this probably doesn't matter — but the approval routing for facilities requests runs through a completely different system than everything else. It's in a SharePoint list that Linda in Facilities manages manually. I don't think anyone on the project knows that exists. Michael's team is only looking at the main approval platform. But about 15% of all approval volume goes through Linda's list. If you replace the main system and don't account for that... you'll have two systems and the problem will be worse than it is now.

*[leaves quietly]*
