# Interview — Peter Gibbons, Senior Process Analyst

**Date:** 2026-03-02
**Interview type:** Simulated stakeholder interview
**Persona:** Peter Gibbons — Senior Process Analyst
**Setting:** Peter's cubicle. He's leaned back in his chair, clearly not in a rush.

---

**Q: Peter, thanks for talking with me. Can you walk me through what you actually do with the approval process day to day?**

**Peter:** Yeah. So... I route things. That's basically it. A request comes in, I figure out who needs to sign off, I send it to them, and then I wait. And wait. And then someone pings me asking where their approval is, and I ping the person who's sitting on it, and they say they didn't see it, so I resend it. That's my day. That's every day.

---

**Q: Sounds repetitive. What's the most frustrating part?**

**Peter:** **The process works fine on paper. The problem is that nobody follows it, including me, and the only reason anything gets approved at all is because three of us have figured out how to route around the bottlenecks.** Like, there's a 9-step workflow Tom designed. On paper, it's beautiful. In reality, I skip steps 4 and 6 on about 70% of requests because they're pointless. Step 4 is a "routing accuracy review" — which is literally Tom reviewing whether I routed it to Tom correctly. And step 6 is a secondary sign-off that was added after one bad purchase order in 2022. One. And now every single request has to go through it.

> *[Contradicts Tom: Tom would insist every step has a purpose and that "people don't understand what I do here." Peter's calling two of those steps vestigial.]*

---

**Q: You mentioned workarounds. What do those look like?**

**Peter:** Okay, so for example — Samir is technically supposed to review and sign off independently. But if I wait for Samir, it takes three to five days because he re-checks everything from scratch. So what I do is, I'll email Samir's counterpart first, get a soft verbal, and then I'll route to Samir with a note that says "Reviewed by [name], please countersign." He signs off in a day instead of five. Everybody's happy, the request moves. Is that the "right" process? No. Does it work? Yeah. Every time.

> *[UNRECOGNIZED REVEAL: Peter is fabricating social proof to accelerate approvals. He presents verbal check-ins as formal reviews to give Samir permission to move faster. This means current cycle time metrics reflect Peter's invisible interventions, not the actual process design. Any new system benchmarked against current performance is being measured against a process that doesn't officially exist.]*

---

**Q: Does Lumbergh know you're doing this?**

**Peter:** Lumbergh doesn't know how the process actually works. He thinks his "just loop me in" emails are a courtesy. They're not. They're a gate. Nothing moves until Lumbergh replies "Go ahead" — and sometimes that takes two days because he's in meetings or he forgot. But if I skip him and route straight to Samir, Lumbergh finds out later and gets weird about it. So now I wait. Everyone waits. For a "courtesy copy" that's actually an approval checkpoint nobody put in the workflow.

> *[Contradicts Lumbergh: Lumbergh says his involvement "doesn't slow anything down." Peter says it adds 1-2 days to every request over $5K.]*

---

**Q: What about the tool itself — is that part of the problem?**

**Peter:** The tool is fine. I mean, it's old, sure. Michael wants to burn it down and rebuild from scratch because that's Michael. But the tool does what we'd need it to do if anyone actually configured it right. It's got parallel routing, escalation rules, delegation — none of it's turned on. The problem isn't the software. The problem is that seven people are touching something that needs three people, and half of them are reviewing each other's reviews. You could give us the best tool in the world and it wouldn't matter if the chain is still this long.

> *[Contradicts Michael: Michael says the platform is the core issue. Peter says the tool is fine — the process is the problem. They're looking at the same broken system from opposite ends.]*

---

**Q: If you could change one thing tomorrow, what would it be?**

**Peter:** Cut the chain. I don't need nine steps. Nobody needs nine steps for a $3,000 software license. Give me three approvers max: budget holder, compliance if it hits the threshold, and done. Right now I'm routing things through Tom, Samir, Lumbergh's inbox, and sometimes the Bobs — and half of them are checking the same thing. It's not governance. It's a trust problem dressed up as a process.

---

**Q: Samir would probably say his reviews are essential for audit readiness. How do you see that?**

**Peter:** Samir's a good guy. He's thorough. He's also re-doing work that was already done because he doesn't trust anyone upstream. And honestly? I get it. The system doesn't show him what prior approvers checked, so he starts from scratch every time. But that's not a Samir problem — that's a visibility problem. If he could see that the budget was already verified at step 2, he wouldn't need to re-verify at step 5. He's adding cycle time because the process gives him no context.

> *[Contradicts Samir: Samir frames his extra review as due diligence. Peter frames it as redundant work caused by poor system design. Both are partially right.]*

---

**Q: Last question. Do you document your workarounds anywhere?**

**Peter:** *[laughs]* No. Why would I? If I document them, someone makes them official, and then Tom adds three more steps to "govern" the workaround. I've seen it happen. It's better if it just... works quietly. The only people who know are me, Joanna's admin, and one person in finance. We've got a system. It's fine.

> *[This means no one can replicate Peter's process if he leaves, and any metrics built on current throughput will collapse without his hidden interventions.]*
