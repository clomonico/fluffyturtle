# Interview — Samir Nagheenanajar, Finance Approver

**Date:** 2026-03-02
**Interview type:** Simulated stakeholder interview
**Persona:** Samir Nagheenanajar — Finance Approver
**Setting:** Samir's desk, meticulously organized. A printed copy of the approval policy is visible under a paperweight.

---

**Q: Samir, thanks for your time. Can you tell me about your role in the approval process?**

**Samir:** Of course. I handle the financial review for all budget-impacting requests. My job is to verify that the numbers are correct, the budget allocation is appropriate, and we have proper documentation before anything gets committed. It's not glamorous, but it's important. One bad approval and you're explaining it to the CFO. I've seen it happen.

---

**Q: How long does a typical review take you?**

**Samir:** It depends on the quality of what I receive. If the request is complete — budget code, justification, supporting docs — I can turn it around in a few hours. The problem is that maybe 40% of what lands on my desk is missing something. Wrong cost center. No vendor quote. Vague justification like "operational need." So I have to send it back, wait for corrections, re-review. That's where the time goes. The bottleneck isn't me — it's the quality of the inputs.

> *[Contradicts Joanna: Joanna sees Samir's reviews as the delay. Samir says the delay is upstream — incomplete submissions. They're each seeing the half of the problem closest to them.]*

---

**Q: Some people we've spoken with feel like there might be too many review steps in the chain. What's your perspective?**

**Samir:** I understand why people feel that way. But I'd ask — which steps would you remove? The budget verification? The compliance check? These exist for a reason. Two years ago someone approved a vendor contract without checking that we had budget remaining in that line item. We were $40,000 over by the time anyone noticed. That's why my step exists. That's why I check everything.

---

**Q: That incident in 2022 — was your review step added after that happened?**

**Samir:** The formal financial review always existed. But after that incident, I started doing a secondary cross-reference against the live budget, not just the allocated amount. It's not in the written policy — it's something I added because I don't want to be the person who signs off on something that puts us over budget. **I'd rather be the guy who slowed things down than the guy who approved the thing that blew up in front of the CFO.**

---

**Q: So that secondary check — is that something compliance requires, or is it your own safeguard?**

**Samir:** It's... both. I mean, the compliance team — the Bobs — they want to see that financial due diligence was done. They don't specify exactly how. So I made it more thorough. Better safe than sorry. It's maybe an extra hour per request, but given the volume, it adds up. I'd say it adds about a day to my average turnaround, but the alternative is approving something blind and hoping for the best. I won't do that.

> *[Note: Samir just acknowledged his secondary review isn't policy-mandated. It's personal risk mitigation that adds approximately one day per request across all requests. He doesn't frame it as optional because to him it shouldn't be.]*

---

**Q: Peter mentioned that sometimes he routes things with a note that someone upstream already reviewed it. Does that change how you handle it?**

**Samir:** Honestly? Not really. I appreciate Peter trying to streamline things, but I can't verify what someone else checked unless I see it in the system. There's no audit trail for verbal approvals. So even if Peter tells me "finance already looked at this," I still need to do my own review. It's not that I don't trust people — it's that the system doesn't give me visibility into what was already validated. If I could see that the budget line was verified at step 2 with a timestamp and a name, I wouldn't need to re-do it at step 5.

> *[UNRECOGNIZED REVEAL: Samir just described how to eliminate his own bottleneck — upstream visibility. If the system showed him what prior approvers verified, with evidence, he'd skip the redundant check. He's adding cycle time because the process gives him no context, not because he wants to. He essentially designed his own fix without recognizing it.]*

---

**Q: One more — for requests under $50K, do you still do the full review?**

**Samir:** Yes. I know the policy technically has a lighter touch for sub-$50K, but I do the same level of review regardless. A $12,000 mistake is still a mistake. And honestly, the small ones are where people get sloppy — they think it's not big enough to matter, so they submit incomplete requests, and that's how errors compound. So yes. Full review, every time. It's the right thing to do.

> *[Note: The policy allows a lighter review for sub-$50K requests. Samir applies the full review uniformly. This is another self-imposed gate that adds cycle time to the majority of requests — most of which are under $50K. He frames it as discipline; from a process perspective, it's scope creep on his own role.]*
