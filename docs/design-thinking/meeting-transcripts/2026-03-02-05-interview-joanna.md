# Interview — Joanna, Department Manager (Requesting Side)

**Date:** 2026-03-02
**Interview type:** Simulated stakeholder interview
**Persona:** Joanna — Department Manager
**Setting:** A small conference room Joanna booked for 20 minutes. She checks her phone twice before we start.

---

**Q: Joanna, thanks for fitting this in. Can you describe your experience with the current approval process?**

**Joanna:** In a word? Unpredictable. My team submits requests — software licenses, vendor contracts, budget reallocations — and they disappear into a void. Sometimes something comes back approved in three days. Sometimes it takes three weeks. Same type of request, same dollar amount, completely different timeline. I can't plan around that. **I feel like I'm shouting into a void every time I submit a request.**

---

**Q: When things take three weeks, do you know where they're getting stuck?**

**Joanna:** Not really. Nobody tells us. I'll ping Peter after a week and he'll say "it's with Samir" or "it's waiting on Lumbergh's sign-off." And then I'm chasing it myself, which is not my job. I don't have time to project-manage every purchase order through a seven-step approval chain. I've got a team to run.

---

**Q: Some of the approvers we've spoken with feel the review steps are essential for compliance and accuracy. How do you see it?**

**Joanna:** I'm sure some of them are. But when Samir is re-reviewing a $1,200 annual license renewal that he approved last year — same vendor, same terms, same amount — that's not compliance. That's theater. What's he checking for? Did the price of Slack go up by fifty cents? Meanwhile my team is blocked for four days waiting for him to "verify" something that hasn't changed.

> *[Contradicts Samir: Samir would call this due diligence on every request regardless of size. Joanna sees it as bureaucratic inertia applied indiscriminately.]*

---

**Q: Have you found any ways to work around the delays?**

**Joanna:** *[pauses]* Look, I'll be honest. For anything under $2,500, we've started just putting it on a department credit card. It's faster, it doesn't require approvals, and my team doesn't lose three days of productivity waiting for someone to click a button. I know it's not ideal. I know procurement probably has opinions about it. But when the alternative is explaining to my director why a project is delayed because we couldn't get a $900 tool approved in time, the card wins every time.

---

**Q: What about bigger requests — things that have to go through the process?**

**Joanna:** Those are worse. For anything over $5K, I've started looping in the other department manager — Rachel — informally. We'll coordinate our requests so that if one of hers is already in the pipeline with the same approvers, we'll bundle ours with it. Kind of a "while you're in there" approach. No documentation, no formal process — just two managers trying to keep the lights on. It works, but it means our requests don't always show up as separate line items. Finance doesn't love that.

> *[UNRECOGNIZED REVEAL: Joanna's team is using credit cards to bypass procurement for sub-$2,500 purchases and informally bundling larger requests with another department. This means the current approval data is incomplete — any metrics, audit trail, or process redesign built on that data is working from a false baseline. There's also untracked financial exposure that nobody in finance or compliance is accounting for.]*

---

**Q: Half the workflow has been described to us as governance. Would you push back on that framing?**

**Joanna:** Half the workflow is organizational anxiety, not governance. Regulatory compliance — fine, I get it. The Bobs need their audit trail. But the other four steps? Those are there because someone made a mistake once and the response was to add a checkpoint instead of fixing the root cause. That's not governance. That's scar tissue. And my team is paying for it every day.

> *[Contradicts The Bobs: The Bobs would frame every control as necessary. Joanna sees a distinction between regulatory mandate and organizational reflex — a line the process doesn't currently draw.]*

---

**Q: If you could change one thing tomorrow, what would it be?**

**Joanna:** Give me a predictable SLA. I don't even need it to be fast — I need it to be consistent. If you tell me every request takes five business days, I can plan for that. What I can't plan for is "maybe two days, maybe nineteen." And for renewals — things we approved last year with no changes — why am I going through a full approval cycle again? Auto-renew it. Done.

---

**Q: Last question. What would make you trust the process enough to stop using the workarounds?**

**Joanna:** Show me where my request is. Show me who's holding it. Give me a timeline. And stop making me re-justify the same $1,200 purchase every twelve months. If you can do that, I'll happily retire the credit card trick. I don't *want* to work around the system. I just can't afford to wait for it.
