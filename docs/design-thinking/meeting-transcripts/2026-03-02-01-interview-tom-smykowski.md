## Tom Smykowski — Middle Manager / Process Owner

**Date:** 2026-03-02
**Interviewer:** Design Research Team
**Persona:** Tom Smykowski (Office Space universe)
**Role:** Wrote the original approval workflow. Considers it his legacy contribution.

---

**Q: Tom, thanks for sitting down with us. Can you start by telling us about your role in the approval process?**

**Tom:** Yeah, so, I designed this process. A lot of people don't know that. They just see the steps and complain about them, but someone had to sit down and figure out who needs to sign off on what, and in what order, and what happens when someone's out of office, and what the escalation path looks like when finance and operations disagree. That was me. I built this thing from scratch in 2019, and honestly, it worked great. It still works. People don't understand what I do here, but if this process didn't exist, you'd have purchase orders going out unsigned, contracts approved by people who don't have the authority — it would be chaos.

---

**Q: That's helpful context. When you hear that the VP says the process "takes too long," what's your reaction?**

**Tom:** Look, I respect Bill, I do. But with all due respect, he's looking at this from thirty thousand feet. He sees a number — "approvals take eleven days" — and he thinks we can just cut steps and get it down to three. But every single step is there for a reason. You take something out, and six months later some contract goes through without legal review, and then suddenly everyone's asking, "How did this happen? Who's responsible?" And then they come find me. So yeah, it's easy to say "simplify it" when you're not the one who has to explain the fallout.

---

**Q: Peter mentioned that some of the approval steps feel like theater — that they don't actually add value. What's your take on that?**

**Tom:** Peter — and I like Peter — but Peter doesn't see the full picture. He sees what's in front of him, the routing, the clicking, the emails. He doesn't see the compliance side, the audit trail, the CYA layer that keeps this organization from getting sued. If it feels like theater to him, that's because he doesn't understand the stakes. I mean, that's not a criticism, it's just a different vantage point. The process is there to protect people like Peter from making a mistake they don't even know they're making. Theater. Come on.

---

**Q: Help me understand the full workflow. Can you walk me through every step from request to final approval?**

**Tom:** Sure, okay. So a request comes in, it goes to the initial reviewer — that's usually department level — and then it routes to finance if it's over a certain threshold, and then there's a secondary review that... actually, hold on. That step might only apply to capital expenditures over fifty thousand, but I think we started routing everything through it after that procurement issue in 2020. Let me think. Yeah, there was a one-time thing where a facilities request got approved without the right sign-off, and we added an extra review loop to catch that. And then it just... stayed. But that's fine, because it's a safety net. It catches things. Probably. The point is, there are twelve steps — well, fourteen if you count the exception paths — and each one is there for a reason, even if I'd have to pull up my original documentation to tell you exactly which reason for which step.

---

**Q: So some steps were added as one-off fixes and became permanent?**

**Tom:** I wouldn't frame it that way. I'd say the process evolved to address real-world conditions. That's what a good process does — it adapts. Could some of those additions be revisited? Maybe. But you have to understand, every time I've suggested we review the workflow, I get told "it's not a priority" or "just keep it running." And now suddenly it's a priority because someone said the word "modern" in a meeting. You know what worries me? **If we rip this apart without understanding why each piece exists, we won't find out what we broke until it's too late. And by then the people who pushed for the change will have moved on to the next initiative, and I'll be the one cleaning it up.** That's what worries me.

---

**Q: If you could change one thing about the current process, what would it be?**

**Tom:** Honestly? I'd want people to actually understand it before they try to change it. Nobody has sat down with me in — I don't know — three years? And asked how this works. They just use it and complain about it. I spent months building this. I documented every decision, every exception path, every escalation rule. And when I hear someone say "let's just buy a new tool and automate it," it's like... you can automate a bad process and all you get is a faster bad process. The value is in the logic, the rules, the institutional knowledge. That's what I bring. That's what I do here.

---

**Q: Is there anything about the process you'd want preserved in a new system, regardless of what changes?**

**Tom:** The audit trail. Non-negotiable. The separation of duties for anything over ten thousand dollars. And honestly, someone needs to own the logic. You can put it in a system, fine, but systems don't know why the VP of Marketing has a different approval chain than the VP of Sales. I know why. It's because in 2020, Marketing had a rogue spend issue and we added a second approver. If you just look at the system, it looks inconsistent. But there's a story behind every rule. Somebody has to carry that context. Right now, that somebody is me.

---

**Q: Last question. If this modernization effort moves forward, what role do you see yourself playing?**

**Tom:** I mean, I should be involved. Obviously. I'm the process owner. I'm the one who — look, I'm not trying to block anything. I'm not one of those people who's afraid of change. I just want to make sure we do it right. And "right" means understanding what we have before we throw it away. I've seen this before. New leadership comes in, wants to make their mark, rips out a process, replaces it with something shiny, and two quarters later the same problems come back plus new ones. I don't want that. I want to be part of the solution. I just... I want to make sure there's still a role for someone who understands the why behind the what. That's all.

---
