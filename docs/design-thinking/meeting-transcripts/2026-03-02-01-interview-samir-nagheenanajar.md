## Samir Nagheenanajar — Finance Approver

**Date:** 2026-03-02
**Scenario:** Approval Process Overhaul
**Role:** Mid-level approver who signs off on budget-related requests
**Interview type:** Simulated stakeholder interview (Design Research)

---

**Q: Thanks for making time, Samir. Can you walk me through what a typical approval looks like when it hits your desk?**

**Samir:** Sure. So a request comes in — usually through email, sometimes through the portal, sometimes someone just walks over — and I pull up the supporting documentation. I check the budget codes, verify the amounts against the current allocation, cross-reference the cost center, and then I check whether the requestor has the right delegation of authority. If everything lines up, I sign off. If not, I send it back with notes.

---

**Q: That sounds pretty straightforward. How long does that usually take?**

**Samir:** It depends. If everything is clean, maybe an hour. But honestly? **Most of what lands on my desk is not clean.** Half the time the budget codes are wrong, or the cost center doesn't match, or someone's submitted under the wrong GL account. So I have to send it back, wait for them to fix it, and then review it again from scratch. That cycle can add three, four days easy.

---

**Q: When you say "review it again from scratch" — you start the whole review over even after a minor fix?**

**Samir:** Absolutely. You have to. If I only look at the one thing I flagged, how do I know something else didn't change? Or that the fix didn't introduce a new issue? I'm the one whose name is on the approval. If audit pulls that record eighteen months from now, they're not going to ask the person who fat-fingered the budget code. They're going to ask me why I approved it. So yes, I restart every time. That's just due diligence.

---

**Q: I've heard from some folks on the requesting side that approvals can feel like they take a long time. Joanna in particular mentioned that her team gets blocked waiting for finance sign-off. What's your take on that?**

**Samir:** Look, I respect Joanna, but she doesn't see what I see. She sees a delay. I see a request that came in with three errors and no backup documentation. If her team submitted clean requests, they'd get approved the same day. The bottleneck is not my review — it's the quality of what comes in. And honestly, if I just rubber-stamped everything to move faster, we'd have a much bigger problem. I've seen what happens when things get approved without proper checks. It's not pretty.

---

**Q: Has that actually happened? Something getting approved that shouldn't have been?**

**Samir:** Not on my watch. But before I took over this function, there was an incident with a vendor payment — wrong amount, wrong account, and it sailed through three approvers because everyone assumed someone else was checking the numbers. That's exactly the kind of thing I'm trying to prevent. So when I add a reconciliation step or ask for a second look from my colleague before I sign off, it's because I know what can go wrong. **"I'd rather be the guy who slowed things down than the guy who approved the thing that blew up in front of the CFO."**

---

**Q: You mentioned asking a colleague for a second look. Is that a required step in the current policy?**

**Samir:** Well — it's not formally mandated for everything, no. The policy requires dual sign-off above $50K. But I do it for anything over $10K, sometimes lower if the vendor is new or the account structure looks unusual. It's just a practice I started. My colleague and I have a good system. He catches things I miss, I catch things he misses. It adds maybe a day, but the peace of mind is worth it. I'd actually recommend we formalize that for the whole team.

---

**Q: Peter mentioned that from his perspective, a lot of the process feels like "theater" — steps that exist but don't actually catch real issues. How would you respond to that?**

**Samir:** Peter is very smart, but he processes things. He routes them. He doesn't carry liability for what he routes. When your signature is the last thing between a bad transaction and a regulatory finding, you develop a different relationship with the process. Theater? No. Every step I do has a purpose. Maybe not every step in the *whole* workflow does — I'll grant him that — but everything in my review exists because at some point, something went wrong and we learned from it. Or could have gone wrong. Either way, I'd rather have steps we can justify than gaps we can't explain.

---

**Q: Last question. If we were designing a new system, what's the one thing you'd want us to get right?**

**Samir:** Audit trail. Full stop. I need to be able to pull up any approval and see who touched it, what they saw, what they changed, and why they approved it. If I had that — a real, reliable trail — I'd honestly be more comfortable moving faster on some of these. Right now, I double-check everything because I don't fully trust that the steps before me were done right. The system doesn't show me what the previous approver actually reviewed. So I end up re-verifying their work just to cover myself. Give me visibility into the upstream checks, and you might find I don't need as many of my own.

---

### Interviewer Notes (out of character)

- **Pain quote:** "I'd rather be the guy who slowed things down than the guy who approved the thing that blew up in front of the CFO."
- **Contradiction with Joanna:** Samir says the bottleneck is input quality, not his review. Joanna sees his reviews as the delay. Both are partially right, but neither sees the full picture.
- **Contradiction with Peter:** Peter calls the process theater. Samir insists every step has a purpose, but then admits some steps in the broader workflow may not.
- **Unrecognized reveal:** Samir's sub-$50K peer review practice is not policy-mandated. He also admits he re-verifies upstream work because he can't see what prior approvers actually checked. He's essentially doing other people's jobs a second time — not because policy requires it, but because the system gives him no visibility. He described the fix himself (upstream transparency) without recognizing he just identified himself as an unnecessary bottleneck if that transparency existed.
