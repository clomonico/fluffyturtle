# Interview — Bill Lumbergh, VP of Operations

**Date:** 2026-03-02
**Interview type:** Simulated stakeholder interview
**Persona:** Bill Lumbergh — Executive Sponsor
**Setting:** Lumbergh's corner office. He's holding a coffee mug and leaning against the doorframe even though it's his own office.

---

**Q: Thanks for making time, Bill. Can you tell me the story of the last approval that really went sideways?**

**Lumbergh:** Yeah, so... there was this one, about three weeks ago? Joanna's team needed a vendor contract pushed through — pretty standard stuff, nothing exotic. And it just... sat there. For eleven days. *Eleven days.* I finally had to walk over to Samir's desk myself and say, "Yeah, if you could just go ahead and approve that, that'd be great." And he did! Took him five minutes. So you tell me — why did it take eleven days to get to that five minutes? That's what I'm talking about. **The process is strangling us and nobody seems to care until I personally walk it down the hall.**

---

**Q: Eleven days is a long time. Where in the process do things tend to stall most often?**

**Lumbergh:** It's downstream. Always downstream. My approval takes — I don't know — a day, maybe two? I'm pretty responsive. But then it hits the mid-level people, the Samirs and the Toms of the world, and it just goes into a black hole. No visibility, no urgency. I think part of the problem is Peter — nice kid, but he's not exactly driving these things forward with a lot of, you know, enthusiasm. He routes them, and then he just kind of... waits. Doesn't follow up. Doesn't escalate.

---

**Q: That's interesting. Peter's the one routing things day to day — have you talked to him about what he's seeing?**

**Lumbergh:** I mean, I don't want to micromanage the guy. That's not my style. But yeah, if you could just... talk to him. I think he'll tell you the tool is fine, everything's fine. That's the problem. He's too comfortable with the workarounds. He's got his little spreadsheet, his little email templates — he's basically built a shadow process around the real one, and he thinks that's normal. It's not normal. We need a real system.

> *[Contradicts Peter: Peter would say the "real" process is so dysfunctional he had no choice but to build workarounds, and that nobody above him wanted to hear about it.]*

---

**Q: You mentioned the tool a few times. How much of this is a technology problem versus a process problem?**

**Lumbergh:** Oh, it's the tool. A hundred percent. The system we're on is — I mean, it was fine in 2019. Tom set it up, it worked for what we needed. But we've outgrown it. We need something modern. Dashboards, mobile approvals, maybe some automation — the stuff everyone else has. The process itself is actually pretty solid. I designed most of the routing logic myself, and it covers all the right checkpoints. We just need a better platform to run it on.

> *[Contradicts Tom: Tom would say the process is fine because he wrote it. Lumbergh says the process is fine because he designed the routing. They're both protecting their own contributions while blaming the tool.]*

---

**Q: Walk me through a typical approval from your perspective. What do you actually see and do?**

**Lumbergh:** So what happens is, a request comes in — could be procurement, could be a budget reallocation, whatever — and Peter routes it to the right chain. I usually get looped in pretty early because, you know, I like to have visibility. I don't always need to formally approve, but I want to see it. So I'll get a heads-up email, I'll take a look, and if it looks good I'll reply-all and say "Go ahead." Then it moves to the formal approvers — Samir for finance, Tom if there's a process exception, the Bobs if it's over the compliance threshold.

**Follow-up: So you're reviewing it before the formal approvers even see it?**

**Lumbergh:** Yeah, just a quick gut check. Keeps me informed. I told Peter a while back, "Yeah, just loop me in on everything over five grand." It's not a formal approval step. It's more of a... courtesy copy. Doesn't slow anything down.

> *[UNRECOGNIZED REVEAL: Lumbergh has inserted himself as an informal gate on every request over $5K. He doesn't see it as an approval step, but functionally it is — nothing moves until he replies "Go ahead." Peter likely waits for that reply before routing forward, which means Lumbergh's "courtesy copy" adds 1-2 days to every mid-size request. He just told us he's part of the bottleneck without realizing it.]*

---

**Q: If we could fix one thing about this tomorrow, what would make the biggest difference?**

**Lumbergh:** Speed. Just... speed. I want to see cycle times cut in half. Right now we're averaging — I don't know the exact number, but it feels like two to three weeks for anything non-trivial. If we could get that down to a week, I'd be a hero. My boss is asking me every month for the ops efficiency numbers and I've got nothing good to show her. So yeah, speed and visibility. I want a dashboard that shows me where everything is, who's holding the bag, and how long it's been sitting there.

---

**Q: Have you tried to fix this before? What happened?**

**Lumbergh:** We did a little initiative about a year ago. Tom put together a proposal to add more routing rules — thought the problem was that requests were going to the wrong people. We implemented it, and honestly? It made things worse. Added two more decision points. But that was Tom's thing, not mine. I think we were solving the wrong problem. This time I want to go bigger. New system, clean slate, modern tooling. That's why I came to you.

> *[Lumbergh distances himself from the failed initiative, but he approved it. He's proposing the same pattern — a structural fix without questioning whether the underlying logic is right.]*

---

**Q: Last question. Who else needs to believe this worked for it to actually stick?**

**Lumbergh:** Joanna, for sure. She's the squeaky wheel, and honestly she should be. Her team gets blocked the most. If Joanna's happy, I know we've moved the needle. Samir needs to feel like audit trail is intact — he won't sign off on anything if he thinks we're cutting corners on compliance. And, look — I need the Bobs to be comfortable. If internal audit pushes back, the whole thing dies. Tom will come around. He always does. He just needs to feel included. Maybe give him a title on the project — "Process Transition Lead" or something. He likes that kind of thing.

*[Lumbergh takes a sip of coffee.]*

You know what, I'm glad we're doing this the right way. Getting input, talking to people. Just... yeah, if you could make sure we move fast on this? I've got a board update in six weeks and I'd love to have something to show. That'd be great.
