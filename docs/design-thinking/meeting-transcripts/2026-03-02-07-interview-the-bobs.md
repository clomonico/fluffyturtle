# Interview — The Bobs, Internal Audit / Compliance

**Date:** 2026-03-02
**Interview type:** Simulated stakeholder interview
**Persona:** The Bobs — Internal Audit / Compliance
**Setting:** A sterile conference room. Both Bobs sit on the same side of the table. They have matching folders.

---

**Q: Thanks for meeting with us. Can you describe your role relative to the approval process?**

**Bob 1:** We don't operate the process. We audit it.

**Bob 2:** We verify that controls are in place, that segregation of duties is maintained, and that there's an auditable trail from request to completion. Our interest is compliance, not throughput.

**Bob 1:** We review the process once a year during the internal controls assessment. Outside of that, we're only involved if something triggers an exception or a threshold breach.

---

**Q: Some stakeholders feel there are too many approval steps. From a regulatory standpoint, how many steps are actually required?**

**Bob 1:** The regulatory framework requires three control points for expenditure approvals: requestor verification, budget authority confirmation, and segregation-of-duties sign-off.

**Bob 2:** Three. That's the regulatory floor. Anything beyond that is organizational policy — internally imposed controls. The current workflow has seven approval tiers. Three are regulatory. Four are elective.

**Bob 1:** We documented this in the control matrix we published eighteen months ago. It clearly delineates mandatory versus elective controls. From our perspective, the data is available.

> *[UNRECOGNIZED REVEAL: Only 3 of the 7 approval tiers are regulatory requirements. The other 4 were added internally over time — by Tom, by Samir, by Lumbergh's informal gate. The Bobs published this distinction in a control matrix that "nobody reads." The process is nearly twice as long as compliance requires.]*

---

**Q: So you're saying the organization could cut four approval steps without any compliance risk?**

**Bob 2:** We're saying the regulatory requirement is three control points. Whether the organization chooses to maintain additional controls is a business decision, not a compliance decision.

**Bob 1:** We wouldn't oppose simplification as long as the three mandatory controls remain intact and the audit trail is preserved. What we *would* oppose is any change that weakens documentation or removes traceability.

---

**Q: Joanna mentioned that her team sometimes uses credit cards for purchases under $2,500 to avoid the approval process. Does that concern you?**

**Bob 1:** *[Bobs exchange a glance]*

**Bob 2:** That concerns us significantly. P-card transactions bypass the approval workflow entirely. If that's happening at scale, it means a portion of organizational expenditure has no approval trail, no budget verification, and no segregation of duties.

**Bob 1:** We'd need to quantify the exposure. If it's isolated, it's a policy enforcement issue. If it's systemic, it's a control gap we'd need to flag in the next audit cycle. **That's exactly the kind of thing that keeps us up at night — not that the process is too slow, but that people are routing around it entirely.**

---

**Q: What about the current process works well from your perspective?**

**Bob 2:** The principle is sound. The request-to-approval chain creates accountability at each level. The problem — and we've noted this in two consecutive audit reports — is documentation completeness. Approximately 60% of approved requests have incomplete supporting documentation.

**Bob 1:** Which means even when something is approved, we can't always reconstruct *why* it was approved. The approval exists, but the rationale doesn't. That's a finding we've flagged twice.

> *[Contradicts Joanna: Joanna wants fewer steps. The Bobs see the bigger risk as insufficient documentation within existing steps. From their lens, the process isn't too long — it's too shallow. Approvers are clicking "approve" without recording what they checked.]*

---

**Q: If you could improve one thing about the process, what would it be?**

**Bob 2:** Enforce completeness at intake. If a request can't be submitted without the required documentation attached, the downstream review steps get faster because approvers aren't sending things back for missing information.

**Bob 1:** That alone would be the single most impactful improvement anyone has proposed in the five years we've been auditing this process. Completeness at intake solves the documentation gap, reduces review cycle time, and preserves the audit trail.

**Bob 2:** And it doesn't require removing any controls. It makes the existing controls work better.

---

**Q: Last question. Are you aware that the current system has features like parallel routing and auto-escalation that aren't configured?**

**Bob 1:** We're aware. Parallel routing wouldn't concern us as long as all required control points are still hit. Auto-escalation would actually help — we've seen requests sit for weeks without movement, which creates its own risk.

**Bob 2:** Our concern isn't the technology. It's the process design. If you reconfigure the system to enable parallel approvals, ensure the segregation of duties is maintained. Don't let the same person serve as both budget authority and requestor verification.

**Bob 1:** Beyond that, we have no objection to modernization. Our requirements are simple: three control points, an audit trail, and documentation. How you get there is a business decision.

---

**Q: One more — you mentioned a control matrix that delineates mandatory versus elective controls. Has anyone referenced that in designing process changes?**

**Bob 1:** Not to our knowledge.

**Bob 2:** It was published to the shared governance folder. We referenced it in both the 2024 and 2025 audit reports. We assumed stakeholders were using it. If they're not, that would explain why elective controls are being treated as mandatory — and why people believe the compliance burden is heavier than it actually is.
