# Interview — Milton Waddams, Long-tenured Specialist

**Date:** 2026-03-02
**Interview type:** Simulated stakeholder interview
**Persona:** Milton Waddams — Long-tenured Specialist / Institutional Memory
**Setting:** Milton's desk — relocated to a half-cubicle near the storage closet. A red Swingline stapler is positioned protectively at the front of his desk. He speaks quietly and rarely makes eye contact.

---

**Q: Milton, thanks for talking with us. We're looking at the approval process and wanted to get your perspective. How long have you been working with it?**

**Milton:** I... well, I've been here since 2011. Before the current process. Before Tom. Before the system. I was here when approvals were done on paper forms. Yellow carbon copies. I still have some of those forms, actually, in a box under my... well, they moved my desk, so I'm not sure where the box is now. But I have them. I remember every form. Nobody asks me about those anymore. Nobody asks me about anything, really. They moved my desk four times. I used to be by the window.

---

**Q: That's a lot of history. Do you have direct involvement in the current approval workflow?**

**Milton:** I... I'm not on the flowchart. Tom made the flowchart and I'm not on it. But they still route things to me. Not officially. Peter sends me the vendor exception requests because I'm the only one who knows which vendors have special payment terms. There are forty-seven vendors with non-standard net terms. I have them in a spreadsheet. Tom doesn't know about the spreadsheet. He thinks the system handles it, but the system only has the standard terms loaded. So Peter sends them to me, and I check, and I send them back. It adds a day, sometimes two. But if I don't check, you get late fees. Nobody wants to talk about that, though. **They didn't even invite me to the kickoff meeting.**

> *[Contradicts Tom: Tom claims the process is self-sustaining and he built exception handling into the workflow. Milton reveals there's a critical step — vendor terms verification — that Tom's process doesn't cover at all. Milton IS the exception handling, and he's not even in the RACI.]*

---

**Q: You mentioned late fees. Has that actually happened?**

**Milton:** In 2019. Before step 7 was added. There was a vendor — I remember because it was the heating and cooling vendor, and my desk was right next to the vent at the time, and the vent was blowing cold air, which I told them about but they didn't... anyway. The payment terms were net-15, not net-30. Nobody checked. The approval went through standard routing, took 22 days, and we hit a $14,000 late fee. Plus a penalty clause. I told Tom about the issue *before* it happened. I sent him an email. He said he'd "look into it." Two months later, when the late fee hit, he added step 7. He told Lumbergh it was his idea. It was my idea. I have the email.

> *[Contradicts Michael: Michael wants to eliminate step 7 as "redundant overhead." Milton reveals it exists because of a specific $14,000 failure that he predicted and Tom ignored. Removing it without replacing the check would reopen that exposure.]*

---

**Q: So step 7 exists because of your recommendation, even though you're not credited?**

**Milton:** That's... yes. That's what happened. Tom took credit. He does that. The 2020 exception routing? That was also... I told him about the duplicate vendor payments in September. He added the exception path in November and presented it as his quarterly improvement initiative. I was not mentioned. I was not thanked. My stapler was also taken during that quarter, which I reported to HR, but they said they'd "get back to me." They have not gotten back to me. **Nobody documents that I'm in the process. I'm not in the flowchart. I'm not in the RACI. I'm not in the system.** But if I stopped checking vendor terms tomorrow, they'd notice within a month. Maybe two months. They'd notice.

---

**Q: Peter mentioned he has workarounds for moving things through faster — like splitting requests to stay under thresholds. Are you aware of that?**

**Milton:** Peter splits purchase orders. Yes. He does it for the $5,000 threshold so it doesn't trigger Lumbergh's review, and he does it for the $10,000 threshold so it doesn't hit the exception routing. I've seen it. The problem is that when you split a PO, it also bypasses the conflict-of-interest check at step 7. That check only triggers on consolidated amounts. So if you split a $12,000 vendor payment into two $6,000 requests, neither one hits the threshold for the vendor relationship review. Nobody catches it. Peter doesn't know about the conflict-of-interest piece — he just thinks he's skipping a slow step. But he's actually skipping a compliance control.

> *[UNRECOGNIZED REVEAL — PART 1: Peter's PO-splitting workaround doesn't just bypass a slow step. It bypasses a compliance control that checks for vendor relationship conflicts. This is a material compliance gap that nobody else in the organization is aware of, because Milton isn't in any official review chain.]*

---

**Q: Is there anything else about the process that you think people are missing?**

**Milton:** *[mumbles, then looks up]* There's the Facilities list. Nobody talks about the Facilities list. When I was in Building C — before they moved me — Facilities had their own approval process running on SharePoint. Completely separate from the main workflow. Capital equipment requests, space modifications, HVAC maintenance contracts — about 15% of all approval volume runs through that list. Michael's team doesn't know about it. IT has no visibility. There's no integration with the main system. The audit trail is... well, it's a SharePoint list. It has a modified date and a modified-by column, and that's about it. I only know about it because my desk used to be next to the Facilities coordinator, before they moved my desk. They moved my desk to Storage B.

> *[UNRECOGNIZED REVEAL — PART 2: 15% of organizational approval volume runs through a completely separate SharePoint list that IT, finance, and compliance don't know about. Milton drops this as an afterthought. Any process redesign that doesn't account for this shadow system will miss a significant portion of actual approval activity — and any audit that doesn't include it has a blind spot.]*

---

**Q: Last question, Milton. If someone came to you and asked "what's the most important thing we should preserve about the current process," what would you say?**

**Milton:** *[quietly]* The institutional memory. The process isn't just steps. It's reasons. Every step has a reason, even if nobody remembers it. Step 3 exists because of the 2018 audit finding. Step 7 exists because of the late fee I warned them about. The exception routing exists because of the duplicate payments I flagged. If you throw all of that away and start over... you'll make the same mistakes. And nobody will understand why, because nobody will remember. Except me. But they'll probably move my desk again by then. Or take my stapler. I was told I could keep my stapler.
