# Interview — Tom Smykowski, Middle Manager / Process Owner

**Date:** 2026-03-02
**Interview type:** Simulated stakeholder interview
**Persona:** Tom Smykowski — Middle Manager / Process Owner
**Setting:** Tom's office. A framed process flowchart hangs on the wall behind him. He's visibly nervous about this meeting.

---

**Q: Tom, thanks for meeting. Can you start by telling me about the approval process and your role in it?**

**Tom:** Sure, sure. So I designed the approval workflow back in 2019. It was a big initiative — took about four months to get right. Before that, you had people approving things on sticky notes, forwarding emails with "OK" in the subject line, no consistency at all. I brought structure. I brought discipline. I mapped every approval type, defined routing rules, built in exception handling. People don't understand what I do here, but without that workflow, this place would be chaos. *More* chaos.

---

**Q: Some of the people we've talked to feel like the process might have too many steps now. What's your reaction?**

**Tom:** Who said that? Was it Peter? *[shifts in chair]* Look, Peter doesn't see the full picture. He sees his routing queue. I see the entire lifecycle. Every step in that workflow exists for a reason. You take one out, you create a gap. And then when something goes wrong — and it *will* go wrong — who do you think gets blamed? Not Peter. Not Lumbergh. Me. Because I'm the one who's supposed to make sure the process works. And it does work. It works exactly as designed.

> *[Contradicts Peter: Peter skips steps 4 and 6 on 70% of requests and calls the process "theater." Tom insists every step is load-bearing.]*

---

**Q: Lumbergh mentioned that a previous improvement initiative — more routing rules — actually made things slower. What happened there?**

**Tom:** That's... that's not a fair characterization. What happened was, we identified that certain request types were being routed to the wrong approvers. My solution was to add conditional routing — if it's this type, go here; if it's that type, go there. It was the right call architecturally. The problem was implementation. Michael's team didn't configure it properly, so you ended up with requests hitting *both* paths instead of one. That wasn't my design. That was execution.

> *[Contradicts Lumbergh: Lumbergh framed it as "Tom's thing" that "made things worse." Tom blames IT execution. Neither is owning the outcome.]*

---

**Q: Walk me through the 9-step workflow. Are all 9 steps original, or have some been added over time?**

**Tom:** The core workflow is seven steps. The other two were... situational additions. In 2020, we had a procurement issue — duplicate vendor payments — and I added an exception routing path to catch that. It was supposed to be temporary, but it turned out to be useful, so we kept it. And then in 2022, after the budget overrun incident, Samir asked for an additional verification checkpoint. So that went in too. But they're all justified. You can't just — you know, you can't just rip things out because someone thinks it takes too long. **If we rip this apart without understanding why each piece exists, we won't find out what we broke until it's too late.** And by then it's on me.

> *[UNRECOGNIZED REVEAL: Tom just admitted that 2 of his 9 steps started as temporary patches — one from 2020, one from 2022 — and became permanent without formal review. He also admitted he'd need to consult his own documentation to explain the rationale for every step. The process has outgrown even its creator's complete understanding.]*

---

**Q: If I asked you right now to walk me through the rationale for each of those 9 steps from memory, could you?**

**Tom:** *[long pause]* I mean, I could give you the gist. For the detailed rationale — the original decision documents, the exception logs — I'd need to pull up my files. I have everything documented. It's all there. I just... haven't had to reference it in a while because the process runs itself. That's kind of the point, right? I built it to be self-sustaining.

---

**Q: What worries you most about this initiative?**

**Tom:** What worries me is that someone's going to come in and say "let's simplify" without understanding what they're simplifying. Lumbergh wants speed. Fine. Everyone wants speed. But speed without controls is how you end up on the wrong side of an audit. I've been here long enough to know what happens when you remove a safeguard and then something goes wrong six months later. No one remembers why the safeguard existed. They just remember who took it out. And that person would be me. So if we're going to change things, I need to be involved. I need to be at the table. Because **people don't understand what I do here**, and if they did, they'd understand why it can't be "simplified" by someone who's never had to explain a process failure to the Bobs.

---

**Q: How would you feel about being involved as a process transition lead — helping design what comes next?**

**Tom:** *[brightens visibly]* I mean... yes. Absolutely. That's exactly what should happen. I know this process better than anyone. I know where the pressure points are, where the exceptions live. If you're going to rebuild, you need someone who understands the foundation. Otherwise you're building on sand. I could — I could document the transition requirements, map the exception paths, maybe even train people on the new workflow. That's... that's exactly the kind of thing I should be doing. Finally, someone gets it.

---

**Q: Last question. Is there anything about the current process you'd change yourself if you had the authority?**

**Tom:** *[hesitates]* I... look, if I'm being completely honest? The exception path from 2020 — the duplicate vendor thing — that could probably be handled differently. It adds a step for *every* vendor-related request, not just the ones at risk of duplication. I've thought about scoping it down, but every time I bring it up, people say "don't touch it, it works." Which is ironic, because that's what they say about the whole process, and now they want to tear it all down. So... yeah. I'd scope that one down. But carefully. Very carefully.
