# Simulated Interview — Joanna (Department Manager, Requesting Side)

**Date:** 2026-03-02
**Persona:** Joanna — Department Manager
**Source request:** "Our approval process is killing us..." — VP of Operations
**Interview type:** Simulated stakeholder interview (Design Research, Stage 2)

---

## Joanna — Department Manager, Requesting Side

**Q1: Tell me the story of the last approval that went sideways.**

> Last month I submitted a request for contractor access to a shared drive. Straightforward, right? Three business days go by, I hear nothing. I ping the system, it says it's "in review." I find out it's sitting with Samir because anything with an external party apparently needs a finance sign-off now. Nobody told me that. My contractor sat idle for a week. I'm paying for a person who can't work because somebody in Finance needs to check a box. That's what "went sideways" looks like — it's not dramatic, it's just slow death by waiting.

**Q2: When you say the process takes too long, what does the timeline actually look like for your team?**

> On paper, they say two to three days. In practice? Eight to twelve business days for anything that isn't trivial. And the frustrating part is you have no idea where it is. **I feel like I'm shouting into a void every time I submit a request.** I submit it, and then I just… wait. No status updates, no notifications, nothing. I end up Slacking people directly to find out if anyone's even looked at it.

**Q3: Where in the process do things stall most often?**

> Honestly, the multi-level review. I get that some things need a second set of eyes, but does every single request need to go through three people? I had a software license renewal — we've renewed the same license four years in a row. Same vendor, same terms, same dollar amount. Why does that still need Samir's sign-off and then a director review on top of it? It should be automatic at this point. Samir would probably tell you that every dollar needs scrutiny. I'd tell you that reviewing the same $1,200 license for the fourth time isn't scrutiny, it's theater.

**Q4: What happens when an approval gets stuck — how does your team deal with it?**

> We work around it. Look, I'm not proud of this, but if I know something's going to take two weeks, I'm not going to let my team sit on their hands. For smaller stuff, we've started using a team credit card and expensing it after the fact. Or we'll get started on work that technically requires an approved vendor agreement and just paper it later. It's not ideal, but what's my alternative? Tell my team to stop working?

**Q5: Are there types of requests your team avoids submitting entirely?**

> Yeah, actually. We stopped submitting anything categorized as "cross-departmental resource requests" about six months ago. The process requires sign-off from both department heads, plus a resource allocation review from Tom's team, plus Finance. Last time we tried, it took 23 days and the project window had already closed. Now we just handle it informally — I'll call the other manager directly, we agree on a handshake arrangement, and we figure out the paperwork later. Or never. It works fine. Honestly, most of the collaboration that actually happens in this company runs on those handshake deals, not the formal process.

**Q6: If we could fix one thing about this process tomorrow, what would make the biggest difference?**

> Kill the redundant approvals. If my request is under a certain dollar amount, or it's a repeat of something that's been approved before, just let it through. I don't need three humans verifying that the same thing we did last quarter is still okay. Give me a fast lane for the routine stuff and save the heavy review for things that actually matter.

**Q7: Some stakeholders have mentioned that audit trails and compliance checks are essential to the process. How do you see that?**

> I get that compliance matters — I'm not saying burn the whole thing down. But I think people hide behind "compliance" to avoid making the process simpler. Half the steps in this workflow aren't there because a regulation requires them. They're there because somebody once got nervous about something and added a checkpoint, and nobody ever removed it. If The Bobs came in and actually audited which steps are regulatory requirements versus which ones are just organizational anxiety, I bet half the workflow disappears overnight.

**Q8: What does "good" look like for you six months from now if we get this right?**

> I submit a request, I get a time estimate immediately, and I can track where it is in real time. Routine things get approved in hours, not days. My team never has to choose between following the process and getting their work done. And honestly? I'd love it if nobody on my team even thought about the approval process. It should be invisible. Right now it's the first thing people mention when I ask what's slowing them down.

## Interviewer Notes (Post-Interview)

- **Direct pain quote:** "I feel like I'm shouting into a void every time I submit a request."
- **Contradiction with Samir:** Joanna views Samir's financial review of repeat, low-dollar requests as "theater." Samir would argue each approval cycle is an independent audit event regardless of prior history. Different optimization targets (speed vs. auditability).
- **Contradiction with The Bobs:** Joanna claims half the workflow exists from "organizational anxiety" rather than regulatory mandate. The Bobs would likely dispute this or argue that even non-mandatory controls reduce organizational risk.
- **Revealed without realizing significance:** Joanna's team has been using credit cards to bypass procurement and running cross-departmental resource sharing through informal "handshake deals" with no paper trail. These workarounds mean the current approval data significantly undercounts actual organizational spend and resource allocation — any audit or process redesign based on current system data is working from an incomplete picture. This also means there is untracked financial and contractual exposure the organization doesn't know about.
